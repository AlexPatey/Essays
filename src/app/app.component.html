Commentary on Genesis

Genesis I.1. "In the firste made God of nouʒt heuene and erthe."

By the first is meant the Father-essence: "He is the Beginning," says Augustine,[1] "the Father is the beginning of the Godhead."[2] This beginning is not temporal, for "the beginning of the Father is primary, not proceeding,"[3] i.e., principial, eternal,[4] "just as in our still told myths, 'once upon a time,' does not mean mean 'once' alone but 'once for all.'"[5] And likewise this first is uncreate, for neither does an invariable property have a variable subject, nor could the Father have been created by another, for else that other would have been the first.[6]

"Of nouʒt" accords with the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad: "In the beginning no thing whatsoever was here";[7] and Chuang-tse: "At the beginning of the beginning, even nothing did not exist"; and the Polynesian Tradition: "Taaroa was his name, he stood in the void; no earth, no sky, no men"; this nothing is the "motionless dark... the incomprehensible nature of God [which we call Godhead],"[8] not the darkness that is the privation of light, i.e., not a privation of relation, for the Godhead is Privation itself:[9] "void... light and darkness [as the privation of light], it is rid of both,"[10] rather the darkness that is the permissive principle[11] of light, in the manner as Non-being is to Being, or as kindling is to fire.

"Made... heuene and erthe" introduces us to the subject of Genesis, viz., the procession of manifestation from the apparent bifurcation of God and Godhead; heaven and earth being the poles of manifestation, for "that what is neither on earth nor in heaven anywhere is nought."[12]

Genesis I.2. "The erthe forsothe was veyn with ynne and void, and derknessis weren vpon the face of the see; and the Spiryt of God was born vpon the watrys."

The Waters, or in our text, "the see," are the possibilities of existence, i.e., materia prima, which, "as it is in itself, is in potentiality to form,"[13] and thus, "derknessis weren vpon [its]... face," for as Godhead is to Being, the Waters are to existence,[14] viz., as a permissive principle and as an inexhaustible well, for "My bounty is as boundless as the sea";[15] "Do not think of the water failing; for this water is without end";[16] "A Fountain ever equally unexhaust, a Sea unbounded...";[17] "Unfathomable as the sea, wondrously ending only to begin again, informing all creation without being exhausted";[18] "In the beginning, this world was merely Water."[19]

"The erthe" is materia signata, i.e., the support or plane or substratum or measure of each of the possibilities of existence, as number is to corporeity,[20] or as body is to soul: "Earth is the support of every world."[21] Empty within, because, like "the see," bereft of form, and thus "neuer is fild with water,"[22] i.e. insatiable, for it is desirous of form.

As God "cannot be a Person without a Nature, nor can his Nature be without a Person,"[23] for Person and Nature imply each other,[24] it follows that the distinction between God and Godhead is "rational, not real."[25] Thus in principle there is no real distinction between God and Godhead, they are "One and One uniting,"[26] and only once rationally distinguished can we say "One became Two."[27] This first rational distinction, i.e., "first formal assumption in Godhead is being... God":[28] "It first said, 'I am'";[29] God affirms Himself because His idiosyncrasy, essence, is Being,[30] His Nature "the possibility of existence, [which resides in his Non-Being and] necessarily involves the fact of existence,"[31] and in this distinction is born the Spirit: "the Nothing bringeth itself into a Will";[32] the Spirit is born upon the Waters.

Genesis I.3-4. "And God seide, Be maad liʒt; and maad is liʒt. And God sawʒ liʒt, that it was good, and deuydid liʒt fro derknessis."

"It first said, 'I am'": this affirmation is at the same time a reflection[33] and illumination,[34] in a sense both appropriate to Intellect and Light,[35] for "when there is gnosis, light shines forth,"[36] and shining forth, He brings all the possibilities of manifestation to act, such that light is divided from darkness, whereby God sees His reflection in the Light.

Just as a bud bursts forth and flowers, without thereby suffering a loss of identity, so the Spirit bursts forth as Light,[37] without thereby suffering a loss of Deity. Therefore we can say that the Spirit, which, antecedent to procession, is identified with the Will, is that self-same Spirit, which, posterior to procession, is identified with the Supernal-Sun, "the Principle of illumination and... the Source of manifested light."[38]

Genesis. I.5. "And clepide liʒt, day, and derknessis, nyʒt. And maad is 'euen and moru, o day."
