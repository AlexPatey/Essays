Commentary on Genesis

Genesis I.1. "In the firste made God of nouʒt heuene and erthe."

By the first is meant the Father-essence: "He is the Beginning," says Augustine,[1] "the Father is the beginning of the Godhead."[2] This beginning is not temporal, for "the beginning of the Father is primary, not proceeding,"[3] i.e., principial, eternal,[4] "just as in our still told myths, 'once upon a time,' does not mean mean 'once' alone but 'once for all.'"[5] And likewise this first is uncreate, for neither does the eternal have a temporal beginning, nor could the Father have been created by another, for else that other would have been the first.[6]

"Of nouʒt" accords with the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad: "In the beginning (skt. agre) no thing whatsoever was here";[7] and Chuang-tse: "At the beginning of the beginning, even nothing did not exist"; and the Polynesian Tradition: "Taaroa was his name, he stood in the void; no earth, no sky, no men"; this nothing is the "motionless dark... the incomprehensible nature of God [which we call Godhead],"[8] not the darkness that is the privation of light, i.e., not a privation of relation, for the Godhead is Privation itself:[9] "void... light and darkness [as the privation of light], it is rid of both,"[10] instead the darkness that is the permissive principle of light, in the manner as Non-being is to Being, or as kindling is to fire.

"Made... heuene and erthe" introduces us to the subject of Genesis, viz., the procession of manifestation from the bifurcation of God and Godhead; heaven and earth being the poles of manifestation, for "that what is neither on earth nor in heaven anywhere is nought."[11]

Genesis I.2. "The erthe forsothe was veyn with ynne and void, and derknessis weren vpon the face of the see; and the Spiryt of God was born vpon the watrys."

The Waters, or in our text, "the see," are the possibilities of existence, i.e., materia prima, which, "as it is in itself, is in potentiality to form,"[12] and thus, "derknessis weren vpon [its]... face," for as Godhead is to Being, the Waters are to existence,[13] viz., as a permissive principle and as an inexhaustible well, for "My bounty is as boundless as the sea";[14] "Do not think of the water failing; for this water is without end";[15] "A Fountain ever equally unexhaust, a Sea unbounded...";[16] "Unfathomable as the sea, wondrously ending only to begin again, informing all creation without being exhausted";[17] "In the beginning, this world was merely Water."[18]

"The erthe" is materia signata, i.e., the support or plane or substratum or measure of each of the possibilities of existence, as number is to corporeity,[19] or as body is to soul: "Earth is the support of every world."[20] Empty within, because, like "the see," bereft of form, and thus "neuer is fild with water,"[21] i.e. insatiable, for it is desirous of form.

As God "cannot be a Person without a Nature, nor can his Nature be without a Person,"[22] for Person and Nature imply each other,[23] it follows that the distinction between God and Godhead is "rational, not real,"[24] and this "first formal assumption [rational distinction] in Godhead is being... God,"[25] hence God affirms He Is because His only idiosyncrasy is Being, His Nature "the possibility of existence, [which] necessarily involves the fact of existence,"[26] and thus "the Nothing bringeth itself into a Will":[27] the Spirit is born upon the Waters.
